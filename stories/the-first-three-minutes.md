There is a question that human beings have asked for as long as human beings have existed. It has been answered by myth, by religion, by philosophy, and by poetry. It was not answered by science until the 20th century — and even now, science can only answer part of it.

The question is: where did everything come from?

Not where did the Earth come from, or the solar system, or the Milky Way. Where did *everything* come from — space, time, matter, energy, the laws of physics themselves. What was there before the universe existed? What caused it to begin?

The honest answer is that we do not know what came before. We do not know if "before" even has meaning when applied to a time before time itself existed. The equations of physics break down at the very first instant — what cosmologists call the singularity — and beyond that boundary, our best tools simply stop working.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1419242902214-272b3f66ee7a?w=1200&q=80](The night sky — every photon of light reaching your eyes has been travelling since the stars that emitted it formed, in a universe that began 13.8 billion years ago from a state of infinite heat and density)

But what happened *after* that first instant — from the first fraction of a second onward — is one of the most precisely understood stories in all of science. We know what the universe was doing when it was one second old. We know what it was doing when it was three minutes old. We know, with extraordinary precision, the sequence of events that transformed a featureless, infinitely hot plasma into the structured, complex, star-filled cosmos we inhabit today.

This is that story.

heading: The Priest Who Proposed the Beginning

The idea that the universe had a beginning was not always accepted. For most of human history, the dominant view — shared by scientists and philosophers alike — was that the universe was eternal and unchanging. It had always existed. It would always exist. There was no beginning and no end.

Even Albert Einstein, when he published his General Theory of Relativity in 1915 and found that his equations predicted a universe that was either expanding or contracting — never static — refused to believe it. He inserted a term into his equations, the *cosmological constant*, specifically to force the universe to stand still. He assumed a static universe was correct and adjusted his mathematics accordingly.

It was a Belgian Catholic priest and physicist named **Georges Lemaître** who took Einstein's equations seriously and followed them where they led.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1506318137071-a8e063b4bec0?w=1200&q=80](The expanding universe — if every galaxy is moving away from every other galaxy today, running the expansion backwards leads to a single moment when everything was compressed into one unimaginably small, hot point)

In 1927, Lemaître published a paper showing mathematically that the universe was expanding. He calculated the rate of expansion from observations of distant galaxies — arriving at essentially the same result that Edwin Hubble would famously publish two years later and receive most of the credit for.

But Lemaître went further. In 1931, he published a remarkable paper proposing what he called the **"hypothesis of the primeval atom"**: if the universe is expanding today, then running the expansion backward in time must eventually reach a moment when all the matter and energy in the observable universe was compressed into a single, extraordinarily dense point. A beginning. A moment of creation.

He called it the day without yesterday.

When Lemaître presented his theory at a seminar at Caltech, Einstein — who was in the audience — stood up, applauded, and said: *"This is the most beautiful and satisfactory explanation of creation to which I have ever listened."*

Not everyone was convinced. Throughout the 1940s and 1950s, Lemaître's theory competed with an alternative: the **Steady State** model, championed by the British astronomer Fred Hoyle.

The Steady State model proposed that the universe was indeed expanding — that evidence was by now undeniable — but that new matter was being continuously created to fill the gaps, maintaining a constant average density throughout infinite time. The universe had no beginning. It had no end. It simply was, and always would be.

Hoyle was a brilliant scientist and a formidable debater. He considered Lemaître's primeval atom theory to be scientifically sloppy, philosophically uncomfortable, and suspiciously close to the religious concept of creation. In a 1949 BBC radio broadcast, he used a dismissive term to describe it — intending it as mockery.

He called it the **Big Bang**.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1446941611757-91d2c3bd3d45?w=1200&q=80](Fred Hoyle intended "Big Bang" as a term of ridicule — a jab at what he considered a scientifically unsatisfying theory. The name stuck. The theory proved correct. Hoyle was wrong about this, though right about almost everything else in his remarkable career)

The name stuck. It has been used ever since — even though it is, in many ways, a misleading description. The Big Bang was not an explosion in space. It was an expansion *of* space. There was no centre from which it exploded, no surrounding void into which it expanded. Space itself came into being, and has been growing ever since.

But the debate between the Big Bang and Steady State was not settled by argument. It was settled by accident, in a pigeon coop in New Jersey, in 1964.

In 1964, two radio astronomers at Bell Telephone Laboratories in Holmdel, New Jersey — **Arno Penzias** and **Robert Wilson** — were using a large horn-shaped antenna, originally built for satellite communication, to map radio emissions from the Milky Way.

They kept encountering a problem. No matter where they pointed the antenna — north, south, east, west, toward the galactic centre, away from it — they detected a persistent, faint background hiss of microwave radiation. It was constant in every direction. It did not vary with the time of day or the season of the year.

They spent months trying to eliminate it. They checked their equipment. They recalibrated their instruments. They climbed inside the antenna and found a pair of pigeons nesting there, coating the interior with what they diplomatically described as "white dielectric material." They removed the pigeons. They cleaned the antenna. The hiss remained.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1543722530-d2c3201371e7?w=1200&q=80](A radio telescope similar to the one Penzias and Wilson used — the horn antenna at Bell Labs that accidentally detected the Cosmic Microwave Background, the oldest light in the universe, mistaken at first for pigeon droppings on the equipment)

Meanwhile, at Princeton University, physicist Robert Dicke had independently predicted that if the Big Bang had occurred, the universe should be filled with a faint residual glow of microwave radiation — the cooled remnant of the intense heat of the early universe. He was in the process of building equipment to search for it.

A mutual acquaintance sent Penzias a preprint of Dicke's predictions. Penzias called Dicke. He described what he had measured — a uniform microwave signal of about 3 Kelvin in every direction.

Dicke hung up the phone and turned to his colleagues. He said simply:

> *"They've got it."*

Penzias and Wilson had stumbled, entirely by accident, onto the most important evidence in the history of cosmology: the **Cosmic Microwave Background** — the afterglow of the Big Bang itself, the oldest light in the universe, filling every cubic centimetre of space, in every direction, at a temperature of 2.725 Kelvin above absolute zero.

The Steady State theory could not explain it. The Big Bang predicted it exactly. The debate was over. Practically on his deathbed, Lemaître was informed of its discovery — a confirmation of his theory. He died in 1966. Penzias and Wilson received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1978.

So what actually happened?

The honest answer begins with an admission: we do not know what happened at time zero itself. At the very first instant — what physicists call the Planck time, roughly **10⁻⁴³ seconds** — the universe was so hot and dense that our current theories of physics break down entirely. General relativity and quantum mechanics — our two best descriptions of reality — become mutually contradictory at this scale. A theory of quantum gravity, which would reconcile them, does not yet exist.

What we can say is this: at time zero, everything that exists — all the matter, all the energy, all the space — was compressed into an unimaginably small region of infinite density and infinite temperature. Not a point in space. Space did not yet exist in any meaningful sense. The singularity was not located somewhere. It was everywhere, because everywhere was the same place.

Then it expanded.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1419242902214-272b3f66ee7a?w=1200&q=80](The oldest light we can detect — the Cosmic Microwave Background — shows us the universe as it was 380,000 years after the Big Bang. Before that, the universe was opaque to light. The first instant remains, for now, beyond the reach of any telescope)

At **10⁻³⁶ seconds** — a decimal point followed by 35 zeros and a 1 — the universe underwent a process called **inflation**: an extraordinarily rapid expansion in which it grew by a factor of at least 10²⁶ in a fraction of a second. In that instant, a region smaller than a proton expanded to roughly the size of a marble. The uniformity of the universe we observe today — the fact that the Cosmic Microwave Background is the same temperature in every direction to one part in 100,000 — is a direct consequence of inflation. Everything we can observe was once in such close contact that it reached thermal equilibrium, and then inflation stretched it to cosmic scales before it had time to develop significant variations.

At the end of inflation, the energy driving it was converted into a seething plasma of particles and radiation. The universe was reheated to almost unimaginable temperatures. The story of the first three minutes had begun.

In the first **microsecond** — one millionth of a second — the universe was a featureless plasma of quarks, gluons, electrons, neutrinos, and photons, all interacting with each other at temperatures exceeding **10 trillion degrees Celsius**. At these energies, no structure could form and survive. Particles were created and annihilated continuously, matter and antimatter appearing and disappearing in an endless cycle of creation and destruction.

This was the **quark-gluon plasma** — a state of matter so extreme that it has only been briefly recreated on Earth in particle accelerators like CERN's Large Hadron Collider, and then only in vanishingly small volumes for fractions of a second.

At around **one microsecond** after the Big Bang, the universe had cooled enough for quarks to bind together into the first protons and neutrons — a transition called **hadronisation**. The quark-gluon plasma ended. The universe was now filled with protons, neutrons, electrons, and an enormous number of photons and neutrinos.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1630694093867-4b947d812bf0?w=1200&q=80](Inside CERN's Large Hadron Collider — the only place on Earth where conditions even remotely approaching those of the early universe have been briefly recreated, giving physicists a window into the first microseconds of existence)

But there was a problem — one that still has no fully satisfying explanation.

For every proton and neutron formed from quarks, an equal and opposite antiproton and antineutron should have formed. Matter and antimatter should have been created in exactly equal quantities. And when matter meets antimatter, they annihilate each other completely, converting entirely to energy.

If the universe had been perfectly symmetric — equal matter and antimatter — they would all have annihilated. The universe would contain nothing but radiation. No atoms, no stars, no galaxies, no us.

But the universe was not perfectly symmetric. For reasons still not fully understood, matter outnumbered antimatter by a tiny margin — approximately **one part in a billion**. Most of the matter and antimatter annihilated, flooding the universe with photons. But that tiny excess of matter survived. Every atom in the observable universe — every star, every planet, every molecule in every living thing — is the descendant of that one-in-a-billion surplus.

We exist because of an imperfection so small it is almost nothing. Almost.

At **one second** after the Big Bang, the temperature had fallen to approximately **10 billion degrees** — hot enough to feel incomprehensible, but cold enough, by the standards of the early universe, for something remarkable to begin.

Protons and neutrons had been smashing into each other since hadronisation, but the energy of collisions had been too high for them to stay bound together — any nucleus that formed was immediately blasted apart by the intense radiation. Now, at one second, the temperature had dropped to the point where nuclear bonds could begin to hold.

The universe had become, in effect, a nuclear reactor.

Over the next **three minutes**, in what cosmologists call **Big Bang Nucleosynthesis**, protons and neutrons fused to form the first atomic nuclei. First: deuterium — a proton and neutron bound together, the simplest possible nucleus beyond plain hydrogen. Then deuterium nuclei fused with protons to form helium-3. Then helium-3 nuclei combined to form helium-4 — two protons and two neutrons, the most stable light nucleus. Trace amounts of lithium-7 were also formed.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1506318137071-a8e063b4bec0?w=1200&q=80](A stellar nebula — the raw material of stars is the hydrogen and helium forged in the first three minutes of the universe's existence. Every atom of hydrogen in every star, ocean, and living cell was created in those three minutes)

Then, at approximately **three minutes**, the expansion of the universe had cooled and diluted the plasma enough that nuclear fusion stopped. The window for nucleosynthesis closed, never to reopen at a cosmic scale.

The result: a universe whose ordinary matter was composed of approximately **75% hydrogen** and **25% helium** by mass, with trace amounts of deuterium and lithium. These ratios — predicted precisely by Big Bang theory and confirmed by measurements of the oldest stars and gas clouds — remain one of the most powerful pieces of evidence that the Big Bang occurred.

Every hydrogen atom in every ocean on Earth, in every star in every galaxy, was forged in those first three minutes. The helium in every balloon, in every star's core, was created then. The universe had written the recipe for its future in the first three minutes, and everything that came after — all the stars, all the planets, all the chemistry, all the life — was a consequence of that recipe.

After those first three minutes, the universe entered a long, featureless period that cosmologists call the **plasma era**.

The universe was filled with a hot, dense plasma of hydrogen and helium nuclei and electrons — all ionised, all free-floating, all constantly scattering photons. Light could not travel freely. The universe was opaque — not dark, but blazing with radiation that was immediately scattered, absorbed, and re-emitted by the dense plasma. If you could somehow have been there, you would have been inside something like the interior of a star — an unimaginably bright, impossibly hot fog.

This lasted for **380,000 years**.

Then, as the universe continued to expand and cool, the temperature dropped to approximately **3,000 Kelvin** — cool enough for electrons to combine with protons and helium nuclei to form the first stable neutral atoms. This event is called **recombination** — though the name is slightly misleading, since it was the first time electrons and nuclei had combined, not a re-combination.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1543722530-d2c3201371e7?w=1200&q=80](A star-forming nebula — clouds of neutral hydrogen just like these formed when the universe first became transparent at 380,000 years old. From these clouds, gravity slowly assembled the first stars over the following hundreds of millions of years)

When recombination occurred, the plasma cleared. Photons — light — could suddenly travel freely through the universe for the first time. The universe became **transparent**.

Those photons are still travelling. Stretched by 13.8 billion years of cosmic expansion from the visible light of a 3,000 Kelvin plasma to the microwave wavelengths of a 2.725 Kelvin background signal, they fill every cubic centimetre of space right now. They are the Cosmic Microwave Background — the afterglow that Penzias and Wilson accidentally detected in their antenna in New Jersey, the oldest light in the observable universe, a photograph of the cosmos at 380,000 years old.

Every time you turn on a television set — old enough to use an analogue aerial — a small fraction of the static on the screen comes from the Cosmic Microwave Background. A little of that snow is the afterglow of the Big Bang.

After recombination, the universe entered what cosmologists call the **Cosmic Dark Ages**.

Not dark in the sense of the plasma era — the universe was now transparent. Dark in the literal sense: there were no stars. The universe was filled with vast, slowly cooling clouds of neutral hydrogen and helium, with no source of light anywhere. If you could have been there, you would have been in genuine, total darkness — no stars, no galaxies, nothing.

Gravity was working. Slowly, the tiny density fluctuations that had been imprinted on the universe during inflation — those one-in-100,000 temperature variations in the Cosmic Microwave Background — were being amplified. Regions that were ever so slightly denser than their surroundings attracted more matter. Gradually, the gas clouds began to contract.

At approximately **200 million years** after the Big Bang — a date that continues to be refined by observations with the James Webb Space Telescope — the first stars ignited.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1462331940025-496dfbfc7564?w=1200&q=80](The Milky Way — every star in this band of light is a second, third, or later generation star, its material forged in the nuclear furnaces of stars that were born and died before our Sun existed. The first stars were made of nothing but hydrogen and helium from the Big Bang)

These were extraordinary objects. Made entirely of primordial hydrogen and helium — the universe had not yet produced any heavier elements — they were enormous, perhaps hundreds of times more massive than our Sun, and ferociously luminous. They burned through their fuel in perhaps a million years and died in cataclysmic supernova explosions, seeding the universe around them with the first heavy elements — carbon, oxygen, nitrogen, iron, silicon — forged in their cores and scattered by their deaths.

The Cosmic Dark Ages ended. The universe was lit.

From the hydrogen and helium of the first three minutes, through the darkness of 200 million years, to the first starlight — the universe had been building toward complexity since its first second. Everything that followed — galaxies, planets, oceans, life, curiosity — was a further elaboration of a process that began in a state of absolute simplicity.

The Big Bang theory, as it stands today, is one of the most successful scientific theories ever constructed. Its predictions — the expansion of the universe, the abundances of hydrogen and helium, the existence and precise temperature of the Cosmic Microwave Background, the large-scale structure of galaxies — have been confirmed by observation to extraordinary precision.

The Planck satellite, whose final data were released in 2018, mapped the Cosmic Microwave Background to an angular resolution of a fraction of a degree, measuring the temperature of the early universe to six significant figures. The agreement between theory and observation is so precise that cosmologists now speak of a **Standard Model of Cosmology** — the Lambda-CDM model — with the same confidence that particle physicists speak of their Standard Model.

And yet the map has edges. Beyond those edges: genuine mystery.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1614732414444-096e5f1122d5?w=1200&q=80](A deep galaxy field — every galaxy visible in images like this is moving away from us, carried by the expansion of space that began 13.8 billion years ago. The most distant galaxies we can see show us the universe as it was less than a billion years old)

**What caused the Big Bang?** We do not know. The singularity itself — time zero — is beyond the reach of current physics. Some models propose that our universe emerged from a quantum fluctuation in a pre-existing quantum vacuum. Others suggest it is one of infinitely many universes in an eternal multiverse. Some propose that time itself is not fundamental — that "before the Big Bang" is a meaningless question, like asking what is south of the South Pole. None of these can currently be tested.

**What is dark matter?** About 27% of the universe's energy content is in a form that does not interact with light but exerts gravitational influence on ordinary matter. We know it is there — the evidence is overwhelming — but we do not know what it is. No dark matter particle has ever been directly detected.

**What is dark energy?** About 68% of the universe's energy content is in an even more mysterious form — a property of space itself that is causing the expansion to accelerate. Einstein's cosmological constant, the term he inserted to keep the universe static and later called his greatest blunder, may have been right after all — for the wrong reason.

We know what 5% of the universe is made of. The rest remains unknown.

Thirteen point eight billion years ago, in a state of infinite heat and density, the universe came into being. In the first three minutes, it forged the hydrogen and helium that would become every star. In 380,000 years, it released the light that still fills space today. In 200 million years, the first stars lit the darkness. In 9 billion years, a medium-sized star formed in an unremarkable spiral galaxy, with a small rocky planet at the right distance to hold liquid water. In 13.8 billion years, on that planet, a creature sat down and tried to understand how it all began.

The physicist Steven Weinberg, whose book *The First Three Minutes* gave this story its name, ended his account of the early universe with a sentence that has never been bettered:

> *"The more the universe seems comprehensible, the more it also seems pointless."*

It is a honest observation. The universe did not begin for us. It does not care about us. The 13.8 billion years of cosmic history that preceded the existence of humanity unfolded in complete indifference to whether any mind would ever exist to observe it.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1419242902214-272b3f66ee7a?w=1200&q=80](The universe as it is now — 13.8 billion years old, two trillion galaxies, expanding and cooling toward a future of darkness. And on one small planet, a species that emerged from the same Big Bang asking where it all came from)

But there is another way to read those same facts.

The hydrogen in your body was forged in the first three minutes of the universe's existence. The carbon was made in the cores of stars that died before the Sun was born. The oxygen, the nitrogen, the iron in your blood — all of it assembled by stellar nucleosynthesis over billions of years, scattered by supernova explosions, gathered by gravity into a planet, organised by chemistry into molecules, and eventually, over four billion years of biological evolution, arranged into a brain capable of asking where it came from.

You are not separate from the universe looking in at it. You are the universe — in one small, improbable corner — looking at itself.

The Big Bang did not happen to you. You happened because of it. The atoms of the first three minutes are the atoms of right now — transformed, organised, and for one brief moment in cosmic time, aware.

That is not pointlessness. That is the most astonishing thing in the history of everything.
