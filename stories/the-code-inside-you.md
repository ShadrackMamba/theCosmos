Right now, inside the nucleus of every one of the 37 trillion cells in your body, there is a molecule about 2 metres long, coiled and folded with extraordinary precision into a space about 6 micrometres across. This molecule — deoxyribonucleic acid, or **DNA** — contains the instructions for building and running a human being.

It is the most remarkable information storage system ever discovered.

## The Double Helix

DNA is structured as a double helix — two long strands wound around each other like a twisted ladder. The "rungs" of this ladder are pairs of chemical bases: adenine always pairs with thymine, and cytosine always pairs with guanine. The order of these four bases — A, T, C, G — spells out the genetic code.

The human genome contains roughly **3.2 billion base pairs**. If you printed them out at font size 12, the sequence would fill a stack of books about 200 metres tall.

## Reading the Instructions

Genes are sections of DNA that code for proteins — the molecular machines that do essentially everything in a living cell. But only about 2% of your DNA is made up of genes. The rest was long dismissed as "junk DNA." We now know much of it has regulatory functions — it controls when, where, and how genes are switched on and off.

The process of turning a gene into a protein involves two steps:

- **Transcription**: The DNA sequence is copied into a messenger RNA molecule
- **Translation**: Ribosomes read the RNA and assemble the corresponding protein, one amino acid at a time

This process happens millions of times per second across the cells of your body.

## You Are Mostly Ancient

Perhaps the most humbling discovery of modern genomics is how much of your DNA you share with other life. You share roughly **60% of your genes with a banana**. About 85% with a mouse. About 99% with a chimpanzee.

The differences between species are not primarily in which genes they have, but in when and how those genes are expressed. The same molecular toolkit, wielded differently, builds everything from a fruit fly to a blue whale.

## The Revolution of Reading

In 2003, the Human Genome Project completed the first full sequence of the human genome after 13 years and $3 billion. Today, a whole human genome can be sequenced in hours for a few hundred dollars.

This revolution is transforming medicine. Genetic diseases that were once mysteries are now understood at the molecular level. Cancer — fundamentally a disease of mutated DNA — is being mapped with unprecedented precision. The era of personalised medicine, where your treatment is tailored to your specific genetic makeup, has begun.

The code inside you is being read, at last.
