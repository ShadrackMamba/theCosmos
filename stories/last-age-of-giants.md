For over 165 million years, the Earth belonged entirely to the dinosaurs. Towering sauropods stretched over 30 metres from nose to tail, their footsteps trembling the ground like slow thunder. In the shadows of vast fern forests, packs of raptors hunted with terrifying precision.

The world of the Late Cretaceous period was warmer, wetter, and more lush than anything alive today. Dense forests stretched from pole to pole. There were no polar ice caps, and lush tropical vegetation covered the entire planet.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1448375240586-882707db888b?w=1200&q=80](Dense ancient tropical forests like this covered every continent during the Cretaceous — home to thousands of species that had evolved over tens of millions of years)

Above warm shallow seas, pterosaurs with wingspans wider than a small aeroplane rode the thermal winds. The diversity of life was staggering — every ecological niche on land, sea, and sky had been filled.

The planet was already under volcanic stress long before the asteroid arrived. In what is now India, the Deccan Traps had been erupting for hundreds of thousands of years — pouring rivers of lava across the land and carbon dioxide into the atmosphere.

The dinosaurs had survived worse before. They were, by nature, survivors. But the planet was fragile in ways they could not know.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1506905925346-21bda4d32df4?w=1200&q=80](Volcanic eruptions similar to the Deccan Traps had been reshaping the planet for thousands of years before the asteroid struck)

Somewhere in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter, a gravitational nudge had sent a massive chunk of carbonaceous rock — roughly **12 kilometres wide** — onto a new orbital path. Slowly, over millennia, that path curved inward toward the inner solar system at nearly 70,000 kilometres per hour.

The dinosaurs had no astronomers, no telescopes, no way of knowing. Nothing but the open sky above them — the same sky they had known for 165 million years.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1419242902214-272b3f66ee7a?w=1200&q=80](The night sky the dinosaurs looked up at — unaware that a mountain of rock was crossing the void toward them)

In the final hours, the asteroid would have appeared as a blinding point of light growing larger on the horizon. Birds and pterosaurs scattered. The sea went eerily still. Then came the impact.

When the asteroid hit the shallow Yucatán sea, the explosion released energy equivalent to **ten billion Hiroshima bombs**. Rock and water at the impact site were instantly vaporised. A fireball erupted outward faster than sound. Everything within a thousand kilometres was incinerated in minutes.

Billion-tonne tsunamis raced across shallow inland seas. Earthquakes far stronger than any in recorded history ripped through the crust of the Earth.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1504701954957-2010ec3bcec1?w=1200&q=80](Wildfires swept across entire continents within hours — triggered by superheated debris raining back down from the stratosphere)

Billions of tonnes of ejecta — superheated rock fragments — rained back down from space across the entire planet, setting forests ablaze worldwide. The sky itself was on fire.

> "The most devastating effect was not the blast itself — but what it put into the sky."

The impact threw billions of tonnes of dust and sulphur dioxide into the stratosphere. Within weeks, this vast cloud encircled the entire planet, blocking the light of the sun. Without sunlight, photosynthesis stopped. Forests began to die.

First the plants. Then the herbivores that ate them. Then the great carnivores. The food web unravelled from the bottom up.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1534067783941-51c9c23ecefd?w=1200&q=80](A sky blotted out for years — no sunlight, no photosynthesis, no warmth. The impact winter lasted a decade or more)

Global temperatures dropped by an estimated **15°C** within months. For the surviving dinosaurs, huddled in a world stripped of food and warmth, there was no recovery. They faded away, one by one, until 165 million years of dominance went silent forever.

Not everything perished. Small, warm-blooded mammals had lived in the shadows of the dinosaurs for 100 million years, rarely growing larger than a badger. Their size, their ability to eat almost anything, and their nocturnal habits gave them a crucial edge.

With the dinosaurs gone, the ecological niches locked shut for 165 million years were suddenly open. Mammals diversified explosively — filling every corner of the planet.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1474511320723-9a56873867b5?w=1200&q=80](Modern mammals — the evolutionary children of the small survivors who outlasted the great extinction 66 million years ago)

Whales returned to the sea. Bats took to the air. Horses, elephants, big cats, apes — and then, just 300,000 years ago, a creature with an insatiable curiosity that would one day dig up the bones of those giants and piece together the tale of the day the sky fell.

We are here, in part, because an asteroid fell 66 million years ago.
