For over 165 million years, the Earth belonged entirely to the dinosaurs. Towering sauropods stretched over 30 metres from nose to tail. Packs of raptors hunted with terrifying precision. Above warm shallow seas, pterosaurs with wingspans wider than a small aeroplane rode the thermal winds.

The world of the Late Cretaceous period — roughly 66 million years ago — was a very different planet. The climate was warmer and wetter than today. There were no polar ice caps, and lush tropical vegetation stretched far into the northern latitudes.

## The Rock That Crossed the Void

Somewhere in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter, a gravitational nudge had sent a massive chunk of carbonaceous rock — roughly 12 kilometres wide — onto a new orbital path. Slowly, over millennia, that path curved inward toward the inner solar system.

The dinosaurs had no way of knowing that a mountain of stone was falling toward them at nearly **70,000 kilometres per hour**.

## The Day the Sky Caught Fire

When the asteroid hit, the explosion released energy equivalent to ten billion Hiroshima bombs. The rock and water at the impact site were instantly vaporised. A fireball erupted outward faster than sound. Everything within a thousand kilometres was incinerated in minutes.

> "The most devastating effect was not from the blast itself, but from what it put into the sky."

## The Long Darkness

The impact threw billions of tonnes of pulverised rock, dust, and sulphur dioxide into the stratosphere. Within weeks, this vast cloud encircled the entire planet, blocking the light of the sun.

Without sunlight, photosynthesis stopped. Forests began to die. The great ecosystems of the Cretaceous collapsed. First the plants died. Then the herbivores starved. Then the carnivores disappeared. The food web unravelled from the bottom up.

Temperatures plummeted by an estimated **15°C** within months. The "impact winter" lasted years — possibly a decade.

## The Survivors

Not everything perished. Small creatures that could eat almost anything and shelter underground had the best chance. Insects survived. Freshwater fish endured. Turtles and crocodilians clung on.

Most significantly, the mammals — small, warm-blooded, largely nocturnal — made it through. With the dinosaurs gone, the ecological niches locked shut for 165 million years were suddenly open. Mammals diversified explosively, eventually giving rise to every large animal alive today.

Including us.
