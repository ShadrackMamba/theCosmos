There are places in the universe where the laws of physics as we understand them simply stop working. Where gravity becomes so extreme that not even light — the fastest thing that exists — can escape. We call them black holes, and for most of history they were considered mathematical curiosities rather than real objects.

We now know they are everywhere.

## What Actually Is a Black Hole?

A black hole forms when a massive amount of matter is compressed into an impossibly small space. The boundary of no return — the point beyond which nothing can escape — is called the **event horizon**. It is not a physical surface. It is a mathematical boundary in space, as invisible and intangible as the equator drawn on a globe.

Cross it, and the laws of causality change. Information cannot escape. Time, as experienced inside, flows in only one direction — toward the singularity at the centre.

## How We See the Invisible

By definition, black holes emit no light. So how do we detect them?

By their influence on everything around them. Gas and dust spiralling into a black hole form a blazing **accretion disc** — one of the brightest objects in the universe, paradoxically created by the darkest. The friction and gravity heat the infalling matter to millions of degrees, and it blazes across the electromagnetic spectrum.

In 2019, the Event Horizon Telescope collaboration captured the first direct image of a black hole's shadow — the supermassive black hole at the centre of galaxy M87, 55 million light years away. The image, famously blurry and orange, was a triumph of global scientific collaboration.

## Time at the Edge

One of the strangest consequences of a black hole is its effect on time itself. General relativity predicts that gravity slows time — the stronger the gravity, the slower the clock. Near a black hole's event horizon, time for an outside observer appears to slow almost to a standstill.

An astronaut falling into a black hole would, from their own perspective, cross the event horizon in finite time. From outside, observers would see that astronaut frozen at the horizon, slowly fading, never quite crossing — a ghost of a moment stretched across eternity.

## Supermassive Black Holes

At the heart of almost every large galaxy in the universe sits a supermassive black hole — millions or billions of times the mass of our sun. At the centre of our own Milky Way sits **Sagittarius A***, a black hole with the mass of 4 million suns.

These cosmic engines shaped the galaxies around them. During periods of active feeding, they become quasars — among the brightest objects in the observable universe, outshining entire galaxies of hundreds of billions of stars.

The universe, it turns out, is built around darkness.
