On a clear night, far from any city, you can see roughly 5,000 stars with the naked eye. They seem countless — a scattered infinity. But every single star you can see belongs to the same galaxy. Our galaxy. The Milky Way.

Beyond those stars, invisible to the unaided eye, lies something that no human knew existed until the 20th century. Something so vast, so numerous, and so ancient that its full reality is almost impossible to internalise:

There are approximately **two trillion galaxies** in the observable universe.

Two trillion islands of stars, gas, and darkness — each one containing anywhere from a few million to several trillion suns, each one separated from the next by voids of space so enormous that light itself takes millions of years to cross them.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1419242902214-272b3f66ee7a?w=1200&q=80](The night sky above — every star you see is inside our own galaxy. Everything beyond is invisible to the naked eye, yet dwarfs what we can see by a factor of trillions)

This is the story of galaxies. What they are. How they formed in the first moments after the Big Bang. How they grow, collide, and die. And what they tell us about the nature of the universe itself — and our unimaginably small place within it.

chapter: Chapter I · What Is a Galaxy
heading: An Island in the Dark
overlayOpacity: 0.50

The word *galaxy* comes from the Greek *galaxias* — meaning milky. The ancient Greeks saw the pale band of light arching across the night sky and named it the Milky Way, not knowing what it was. For millennia, it was simply a feature of the sky, like the moon or the constellations.

It was not until 1610 that Galileo turned his telescope on that pale band and discovered the truth: it was not a cloud or a vapour. It was made of stars. Countless individual stars, too faint and too numerous to distinguish with the naked eye, blurring together into a luminous smear across the heavens.

But even Galileo could not have imagined the full picture.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1462331940025-496dfbfc7564?w=1200&q=80](The Milky Way arches overhead — not a cloud, but hundreds of billions of individual stars, each one a sun, many with their own planetary systems)

A galaxy is, at its most basic, a gravitationally bound system of stars, stellar remnants, interstellar gas, dust, and dark matter. The word *system* is almost comically inadequate. Our Milky Way contains an estimated **250 to 400 billion stars**. It is approximately 100,000 light-years in diameter. If you were to travel at the speed of light — 300,000 kilometres per second, the fastest anything in the universe can move — it would take you 100,000 years to cross it from one edge to the other.

And the Milky Way is not particularly large, as galaxies go. It is a medium-sized spiral galaxy — respectable, unremarkable. There are galaxies in the observable universe that dwarf it by a factor of ten.

heading: The Great Debate
overlayOpacity: 0.52

For much of the 19th and early 20th centuries, astronomers argued fiercely about whether the faint fuzzy patches they saw through their telescopes — called "spiral nebulae" — were clouds of gas within our own Milky Way, or whether they were entirely separate, distant systems of stars.

The question sounds trivial. It was anything but. The answer would determine the size of the entire universe.

In 1920, the argument reached its climax in what became known as the Great Debate — a formal confrontation between Harlow Shapley, who believed the Milky Way was essentially the entire universe, and Heber Curtis, who argued that the spiral nebulae were distant island universes of their own.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1446941611757-91d2c3bd3d45?w=1200&q=80](The spiral structure of a distant galaxy — objects like this were the subject of one of the greatest debates in the history of astronomy: were they clouds nearby, or entire universes far away?)

The debate was resolved in 1924 by Edwin Hubble, who used the giant 100-inch Hooker telescope at Mount Wilson Observatory to identify individual Cepheid variable stars — stars that pulsate at a known rate that reveals their true brightness — in the Andromeda nebula. By comparing their apparent brightness to their known true brightness, he calculated the distance.

Andromeda was not a cloud of gas inside our galaxy. It was nearly a million light-years away — far outside the Milky Way. It was an entirely separate galaxy, roughly comparable in size to our own.

The universe had just expanded, in human understanding, by an almost incomprehensible factor. The Milky Way was not the universe. It was one island among many. And those islands, it would turn out, numbered in the trillions.

To understand how galaxies formed, you have to go back to the very beginning — to the first moments after the Big Bang, 13.8 billion years ago.

In the first fraction of a second, the universe was unimaginably hot and dense — a state of matter so extreme that none of the elements we know existed. There were no atoms, no molecules, no stars. There was only a seething plasma of quarks, electrons, and photons, all interacting so intensely that even light could not travel freely through it.

As the universe expanded and cooled, protons and neutrons formed. Then, about 380,000 years after the Big Bang, the temperature dropped low enough for electrons to combine with protons to form the first hydrogen atoms. The plasma cleared. For the first time, light could travel freely through the universe.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1506318137071-a8e063b4bec0?w=1200&q=80](The cosmic microwave background — the oldest light in the universe, the faint afterglow of the Big Bang itself, shows the tiny temperature fluctuations that would eventually become galaxies)

That first light — now cooled to a faint microwave glow that permeates every cubic centimetre of space — is called the Cosmic Microwave Background. It was detected in 1965 and remains one of the most important discoveries in the history of science.

But embedded in that ancient light are the seeds of everything that came after. Tiny fluctuations in temperature — temperature differences of just one part in 100,000 — mark regions where matter was ever so slightly denser than its surroundings. Over millions of years, gravity amplified these differences. Denser regions attracted more matter. The denser they became, the stronger their gravity. The stronger their gravity, the more matter they attracted.

The first structures in the universe were being assembled.

For the first 200 to 400 million years after the Big Bang, the universe was dark. Not dark as in *night* — dark as in no stars at all. Hydrogen and helium gas, slowly collapsing under gravity, had not yet formed the first stars. Cosmologists call this era the Cosmic Dark Ages.

Then, in the gravitational cores of the densest collapsing clouds, the first stars ignited.

These were unlike any stars that exist today. Made almost entirely of hydrogen and helium — the universe had not yet had time to forge heavier elements — they were enormous, perhaps hundreds of times more massive than our Sun, and ferociously hot. They lived fast and died young, burning through their nuclear fuel in just a million years before exploding as supernovae, seeding the universe around them with the first heavy elements: carbon, oxygen, iron, silicon.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1543722530-d2c3201371e7?w=1200&q=80](A star-forming nebula — clouds of gas and dust collapsing under gravity to form new stars, the same process that created the universe's first galaxies from the primordial darkness)

The explosion of these first massive stars also emitted radiation intense enough to ionise the hydrogen gas filling the surrounding universe — a process called Reionisation, which ended the Cosmic Dark Ages and made the universe transparent to starlight for the first time since those first 380,000 years.

Around the remnants of the first stars, the first proto-galaxies were assembling. Small, irregular collections of stars and gas, bound together by gravity and by the invisible scaffolding of dark matter — a mysterious substance that neither emits nor absorbs light, but whose gravitational influence shaped the large-scale structure of the universe.

heading: Spirals, Ellipticals, and Irregulars
overlayOpacity: 0.48

Look at enough galaxies and patterns emerge. They are not random blobs. They fall into distinct categories — shapes that reflect their history, their environment, and the physical processes that built them.

**Spiral galaxies** are perhaps the most beautiful. A bright central bulge of older, redder stars, surrounded by a flattened disc in which younger, bluer stars trace out sweeping spiral arms. Our Milky Way is a barred spiral — the arms emerge not directly from the centre but from the ends of an elongated bar of stars running through the nucleus. The Andromeda Galaxy, our nearest large neighbour, is a classic spiral. So is the Whirlpool Galaxy, M51, whose two interacting arms — it is in the early stages of merging with a smaller companion — make it one of the most photographed objects in the night sky.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1614732414444-096e5f1122d5?w=1200&q=80](A spiral galaxy seen face-on — the sweeping arms are regions of active star formation, lit by the blue-white light of young massive stars born from collapsing gas clouds)

**Elliptical galaxies** are fundamentally different. They contain few young stars and almost no gas or dust for forming new ones — they are, in a sense, *finished*. Their stars move in random orbits rather than the organised rotation of a spiral disc, giving them a smooth, featureless, egg-like appearance. They range from small dwarf ellipticals to the most massive galaxies in the known universe — giant ellipticals at the centres of galaxy clusters, containing trillions of stars, formed from the mergers of countless smaller galaxies over billions of years.

**Irregular galaxies** follow no pattern at all. They are the chaos of the galaxy world — distorted, asymmetrical, often the result of a recent collision or gravitational interaction. The Large and Small Magellanic Clouds, visible to the naked eye from the southern hemisphere, are irregular dwarf galaxies currently orbiting the Milky Way and being gradually torn apart by its gravity.

Galaxies, for all their vast emptiness, are not safe. They move through space — and space is not uniform. Clusters and superclusters of galaxies are bound by gravity, flowing toward each other across cosmic timescales. And sometimes, two galaxies meet.

Galaxy collisions are among the most spectacular events in the universe. They unfold over hundreds of millions of years, invisible to any individual lifetime — but the geological record of the universe preserves their aftermath in distorted shapes, tidal tails of stars flung outward, and the ignition of star formation on a massive scale.

When two galaxies collide, the stars within them almost never actually hit each other. The distances between individual stars are so vast — even within a galaxy — that two galaxies can pass directly through each other and most stars will feel nothing but a change in their orbit. But the gas clouds — the raw material from which stars form — do collide. They compress. They heat. They collapse. And from the collision erupts a burst of new star formation, called a **starburst**, that can outshine the combined light of hundreds of billions of older stars.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1630694093867-4b947d812bf0?w=1200&q=80](Two galaxies in the process of merging — a process that takes hundreds of millions of years and triggers violent bursts of new star formation across both systems)

The Milky Way itself is heading toward a collision. The Andromeda Galaxy, 2.5 million light-years away, is moving toward us at approximately 110 kilometres per second. In approximately **4.5 billion years**, the two galaxies will begin to merge. The collision will unfold over billions of years, distorting both galaxies beyond recognition, triggering waves of star formation, and ultimately producing a single, large elliptical galaxy that astronomers have already named *Milkomeda*.

Our Sun will almost certainly survive. It will simply find itself in a new orbit within a very different galaxy.

At the heart of almost every large galaxy in the universe lurks a supermassive black hole — an object containing millions or billions of times the mass of our Sun, compressed into a region smaller than our solar system.

Our own Milky Way harbours one: **Sagittarius A***, four million solar masses, sitting at the galaxy's centre 26,000 light-years from Earth. In 2022, the Event Horizon Telescope collaboration released the first image of its shadow — a dark void surrounded by a glowing ring of superheated gas, confirming what decades of indirect evidence had suggested.

Most of the time, Sagittarius A* is quiet. It has little gas to consume. But look at other galaxies — particularly the early universe — and you see central black holes in violent feeding frenzies. These **Active Galactic Nuclei**, or AGN, are among the most energetic phenomena in the universe. In their most extreme form — the **quasar** — a single central black hole, consuming gas at a prodigious rate, can outshine an entire galaxy of hundreds of billions of stars.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1462331940025-496dfbfc7564?w=1200&q=80](The galactic centre — deep within clouds of gas and dust, 26,000 light-years away, a black hole four million times the mass of our Sun holds the entire Milky Way in gravitational thrall)

The relationship between a galaxy and its central black hole is one of the most active areas of research in modern astronomy. The two seem to co-evolve — the mass of a galaxy's central black hole is tightly correlated with the size of its central bulge of stars. How a black hole and a galaxy "know about" each other across distances of tens of thousands of light-years remains one of the deepest unsolved questions in astrophysics.

We live inside a galaxy — which makes it uniquely difficult to study. We cannot step outside and see it face-on. We are embedded within it, like a fish trying to draw a map of the ocean from the inside.

What we know about the Milky Way has been pieced together over centuries of increasingly precise observations. It is a barred spiral galaxy approximately **100,000 light-years in diameter**, containing an estimated 250-400 billion stars. Its central bar is about 27,000 light-years long. It has four major spiral arms — the Perseus Arm, the Scutum-Centaurus Arm, the Norma Arm, and the Cygnus Arm — and several smaller ones. Our Sun sits in a minor spur called the Orion Arm, approximately 26,000 light-years from the galactic centre — well out in the suburbs.

The Milky Way is surrounded by a **galactic halo** — a vast spherical region extending hundreds of thousands of light-years in every direction, containing old stars, globular clusters, and dark matter. The halo is largely invisible, but its gravitational influence is essential: without the additional mass of dark matter in the halo, the stars in the outer disc would fly apart.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1446941611757-91d2c3bd3d45?w=1200&q=80](Looking toward the galactic centre — the dense, bright core of the Milky Way rising above the horizon, a view that humans have looked up at for as long as there have been humans to look)

The Milky Way is part of a small cluster of roughly 50 galaxies called the **Local Group**, dominated by the Milky Way and Andromeda. The Local Group, in turn, is part of the **Virgo Supercluster**, a vast structure containing hundreds of galaxy groups and clusters spread across 110 million light-years. The Virgo Supercluster is itself part of a larger structure: **Laniakea** — a supercluster of superclusters containing 100,000 galaxies and spanning 520 million light-years.

At no scale does it stop being astonishing.

In December 1995, astronomers pointed the Hubble Space Telescope at a patch of sky near the handle of the Big Dipper — a region chosen specifically because it appeared to be completely empty. No known stars. No known galaxies. Just darkness.

They left it pointing at that darkness for ten consecutive days, collecting every photon of light that fell on its detectors.

What came back changed astronomy.

The Hubble Deep Field image revealed not emptiness, but thousands of galaxies — each one in that tiny patch of darkness, each one billions of light-years away, each one a universe of hundreds of billions of stars. The patch of sky covered was about one thirteen-millionth of the entire sky. Extrapolated across the full sky, the image implied the existence of hundreds of billions of galaxies in the observable universe alone.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1506318137071-a8e063b4bec0?w=1200&q=80](A deep field image — in every direction you point a telescope, behind every apparent patch of darkness, thousands of entire galaxies await, each containing billions of suns)

Later, even deeper observations — the Hubble Ultra Deep Field, the Hubble Extreme Deep Field — refined this number upward. The James Webb Space Telescope, launched in December 2021, has since peered even further into the early universe, capturing galaxies that formed when the universe was less than 400 million years old — and finding, to cosmologists' surprise, that many of them were far larger and more mature than the standard models predicted.

The universe, it turns out, built itself faster than we thought. The galaxies were there earlier, were larger earlier, had formed more stars earlier, than our best models allowed. Understanding why is one of the defining challenges of 21st-century astronomy.

In 1929, Edwin Hubble made a second discovery that would prove even more consequential than his identification of Andromeda as a separate galaxy.

He noticed that almost every galaxy beyond our Local Group was **moving away from us**. Not just moving — but moving faster the further away it was. A galaxy twice as far away was moving away twice as fast. Three times as far, three times as fast.

This was not because we were at the centre of some cosmic explosion, with everything fleeing outward from our position. It was because **space itself was expanding**. Every point in the universe was moving away from every other point, carried apart by the expansion of the fabric of spacetime. Like dots on the surface of a balloon being inflated — no dot is at the centre, but every dot moves away from every other dot as the surface expands.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1630694093867-4b947d812bf0?w=1200&q=80](Galaxies in a deep cluster — not static, but in motion, carried apart by the expansion of space itself, their light redshifted toward longer wavelengths by the stretching of the cosmos)

Run the expansion backward, and all the galaxies, all the matter, all the energy in the observable universe converges on a single point of infinite density: the Big Bang. Run it forward, and you get the fate of the universe — a future in which the expansion, accelerated by the mysterious force called **dark energy**, eventually carries all galaxies beyond any possible horizon of observation. In the far future, trillions of years from now, the universe will be dark and empty — all galaxies fled beyond reach, all stars long dead, nothing but cold black holes and fading radiation.

We live in a fortunate era. The universe is lit. The galaxies are visible. The evidence of our origins is still legible in the sky.

There is a question that haunts astronomy, that no one can yet answer but that the existence of two trillion galaxies makes impossible to avoid:

Are we alone?

Our galaxy alone contains perhaps 400 billion stars. Current estimates suggest that most stars have planets. The number of potentially habitable planets in the Milky Way alone has been estimated at anywhere from tens of billions to hundreds of billions. Multiply that by two trillion galaxies, and the numbers become so large they lose all meaning.

The physicist Enrico Fermi asked, famously, if intelligent life is so probable, why haven't we detected it? Why is the universe silent? The question, called the Fermi Paradox, has generated hundreds of proposed answers, none of them definitively satisfying.

::photo[https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1446941611757-91d2c3bd3d45?w=1200&q=80](Our home galaxy, rising above Earth — somewhere in this band of 400 billion stars, on a small rocky planet orbiting an ordinary star, something improbable happened: life became curious about where it came from)

Perhaps life is rarer than we think. Perhaps the distances are simply too vast, the timescales too long, for any two civilisations to overlap. Perhaps the universe is full of life that has simply never found a way to shout loud enough for anyone else to hear.

Or perhaps — and this is the possibility that seems increasingly supported by the evidence from Webb and other telescopes — we are genuinely early. The universe is 13.8 billion years old, but it is expected to support stellar life for trillions of years to come. We may be among the first civilisations in the history of the cosmos to look up at the sky and ask what it is.

If so, the two trillion galaxies out there are not an answer. They are a question — addressed, for now, to us alone.

Step outside on a clear night. Find a dark place, away from the city lights. Let your eyes adjust. The band of faint light arching overhead — that smear of ancient photons — is home. Four hundred billion stars, our galaxy, our island.

And beyond it, in every direction, too faint to see but everywhere: two trillion more.

All of them waiting to be understood.
